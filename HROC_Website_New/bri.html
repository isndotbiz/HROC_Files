üíô Heart of the Community

Why Bri's Work Matters

Bri's love for the community isn't abstract - it's tangible, immediate, and life-saving. On any given day, you'll find Bri on the streets at 2 AM, delivering naloxone and hope to people society has forgotten. You'll hear Bri's voice at city council meetings, demanding that policymakers listen to the people with lived experience. You'll see Bri sitting in circle with community members, holding space for grief, celebrating small victories, and building the trust that makes healing possible.

This deep community connection isn't a job requirement for Bri - it's who Bri is. Bri has walked the same streets, faced the same struggles, and survived the same systems that our community members navigate every day. That shared experience creates a bond of trust that can't be manufactured or taught in any training. When Bri says "I see you, I hear you, and you matter," people believe it - because they know Bri has been there.

üåç Community First, Always

Every program at HROC bears Bri's fingerprints - and more importantly, the fingerprints of the community members Bri listens to relentlessly. When designing our mobile outreach routes, Bri doesn't look at maps and demographics. Bri talks to the people who sleep in those encampments, asks where they need us to show up, and ensures we're there - consistently, reliably, without judgment.

When choosing which harm reduction supplies to stock, Bri doesn't rely on textbooks or national guidelines. Bri asks the people who will use them: What do you need? What would make you safer? What would help you survive tonight, this week, this winter? And then Bri fights like hell to make sure those needs are met.

This is what community-centered leadership actually looks like. Not tokenism. Not performative inclusion. But genuine, deep, reciprocal relationships built on trust, shared struggle, and mutual care. The community isn't HROC's "target population" - the community IS HROC, and Bri ensures we never forget that.

üíö Stories from the Streets

There are countless stories of lives saved because Bri showed up. The person who was ready to give up, until Bri sat with them for three hours in the rain, just listening. The young parent who finally accessed treatment because Bri connected them with culturally-appropriate services that honored their Indigenous identity. The elder who found community again after years of isolation, welcomed into HROC's circle with dignity and respect.

These aren't statistics to Bri - they're relatives, friends, fellow community members who deserve every chance to thrive. And Bri ensures that HROC's culture reflects this truth: that every person who comes through our doors is treated like the sacred, valuable, irreplaceable human being they are.

"I don't separate 'my community' from 'my work' - because they're the same thing. Every person we serve is family. Every life we touch matters infinitely. This is how our ancestors taught us to care for each other, and it's how we'll continue to heal together." - Bri

üìñ Bri's Story & Vision

A Life in Service

Bri's journey in harm reduction spans decades, rooted in deep personal and community experience. With a profound commitment to Indigenous sovereignty and community healing, Bri has dedicated their life to creating safer, more dignified spaces for marginalized communities.

Before co-founding HROC, Bri worked extensively in harm reduction, peer support, and community organizing - always centered on the principle that those most impacted by policies should be at the center of creating solutions.

üå± Core Values in Action

Peer-Led Approaches: Bri believes in the power of lived experience. Every program at HROC is designed with and led by people with direct experience of substance use, homelessness, and harm reduction.

Indigenous Wisdom: Rather than importing outside models, Bri centers Indigenous knowledge systems, cultural healing practices, and traditional approaches to community care.

Dignity First: Every interaction is approached with respect for the inherent dignity and worth of every person. This shapes everything from outreach practices to governance structures.
