üíô Heart of the Community

Why Bri's Work Matters

Bri's love for the community isn't abstract - it's tangible, immediate, and life-saving. On any given day, you'll find Bri on the streets at 2 AM, delivering naloxone and hope to people society has forgotten. You'll hear Bri's voice at city council meetings, demanding that policymakers listen to the people with lived experience. You'll see Bri sitting in circle with community members, holding space for grief, celebrating small victories, and building the trust that makes healing possible.

This deep community connection isn't a job requirement for Bri - it's who Bri is. Bri has walked the same streets, faced the same struggles, and survived the same systems that our community members navigate every day. That shared experience creates a bond of trust that can't be manufactured or taught in any training. When Bri says "I see you, I hear you, and you matter," people believe it - because they know Bri has been there.

üåç Community First, Always

Every program at HROC bears Bri's fingerprints - and more importantly, the fingerprints of the community members Bri listens to relentlessly. When designing our mobile outreach routes, Bri doesn't look at maps and demographics. Bri talks to the people who sleep in those encampments, asks where they need us to show up, and ensures we're there - consistently, reliably, without judgment.

When choosing which harm reduction supplies to stock, Bri doesn't rely on textbooks or national guidelines. Bri asks the people who will use them: What do you need? What would make you safer? What would help you survive tonight, this week, this winter? And then Bri fights like hell to make sure those needs are met.

This is what community-centered leadership actually looks like. Not tokenism. Not performative inclusion. But genuine, deep, reciprocal relationships built on trust, shared struggle, and mutual care. The community isn't HROC's "target population" - the community IS HROC, and Bri ensures we never forget that.

üíö Stories from the Streets

There are countless stories of lives saved because Bri showed up. The person who was ready to give up, until Bri sat with them for three hours in the rain, just listening. The young parent who finally accessed treatment because Bri connected them with culturally-appropriate services that honored their Indigenous identity. The elder who found community again after years of isolation, welcomed into HROC's circle with dignity and respect.

These aren't statistics to Bri - they're relatives, friends, fellow community members who deserve every chance to thrive. And Bri ensures that HROC's culture reflects this truth: that every person who comes through our doors is treated like the sacred, valuable, irreplaceable human being they are.

"I don't separate 'my community' from 'my work' - because they're the same thing. Every person we serve is family. Every life we touch matters infinitely. This is how our ancestors taught us to care for each other, and it's how we'll continue to heal together." - Bri

üíô 24/7 Community Dedication

Bri doesn't keep office hours because crisis doesn't keep office hours. When someone overdoses at 3 AM, Bri is there with naloxone and a steady hand. When the shelter turns people away in the rain, Bri shows up with tarps, blankets, and the kind of presence that says: you matter, you belong, I'm not leaving.

Last winter, Bri spent 72 hours straight on the streets during the ice storm while agencies closed their doors. She moved between encampments with hand warmers, propane, and hot food‚Äîchecking pulses, preventing frostbite, making sure everyone had a plan to survive the night. When others said it was too dangerous to go out, Bri said our relatives were already out there and that's exactly where she needed to be. She didn't sleep until every single person she'd checked on had made it through.

This isn't burnout waiting to happen‚Äîthis is love in action. Bri's community knows that when everyone else disappears, Bri will be there. That knowledge alone has saved lives, has kept people from giving up, has proven that somebody actually gives a damn. That's not a service model. That's family.

üå± Indigenous Wisdom & Healing

Bri brings ceremony into the streets, not as performance but as practice. Sage bundles and tobacco offerings aren't add-ons to harm reduction‚Äîthey ARE harm reduction, reconnecting people to land, to ancestors, to the parts of themselves that colonization and addiction tried to destroy. Bri knows that healing isn't just about staying alive. It's about remembering who you are.

Every month, Bri hosts talking circles at encampments where community members share stories, grief, and hope around a sacred fire. These aren't therapy sessions run by outsiders‚Äîthey're Indigenous-led spaces where people practice traditional ways of being together. Bri teaches younger community members how to prepare medicines, how to smudge, how to carry teachings forward. When someone says they feel disconnected from their heritage, Bri doesn't refer them somewhere else. She sits with them, shares what she knows, and helps them find their way back to belonging.

Bri understands that addiction often fills the hole left by cultural genocide. By weaving traditional practices into every aspect of her work, she offers people a path to healing that honors who they are and where they come from. This isn't culture as decoration. This is culture as medicine, as resistance, as survival.

Bri's lived experience isn't a credential she lists on a resume‚Äîit's the foundation of every relationship she builds. Bri doesn't lead from above‚Äîshe leads from beside, from within, from the knowledge that we're all just trying to make it through. That kind of leadership transforms people because it reminds them they were never broken. They were always whole, always worthy, always enough.

Bri doesn't just advocate‚Äîshe fights, fiercely and fearlessly, in city council chambers and funder meetings and anywhere people try to reduce her community to statistics or problems to be managed. Bri's advocacy isn't about being polite or palatable. It's about refusing to let anyone forget that these are lives, not issues. Every time Bri speaks truth to power, she's saying: my community deserves dignity, deserves respect, deserves a seat at every table where decisions are made about our lives.

Trust isn't given‚Äîit's earned through showing up, again and again, when words fail and systems collapse. Bri has earned trust by being consistent, by following through, by never making promises she can't keep and always keeping the promises she makes.
