What is Education & Training?

Knowledge saves lives. When people understand how to recognize overdose, administer naloxone, practice safer substance use, and navigate health systems, they become powerful agents of change in their own communities. Education and training are at the heart of harm reduction‚Äînot just providing supplies and services, but empowering people with the knowledge and skills to protect themselves, support others, and advocate for evidence-based care.

Healing Roots Outreach Collective offers comprehensive education and training programs designed to build capacity, reduce stigma, and expand access to life-saving harm reduction knowledge. We provide naloxone training and certification, harm reduction workshops, community health education, peer educator development, CPR and first aid instruction, and professional trainings for healthcare providers, social workers, and community organizations.

Our approach centers accessibility and cultural relevance. We deliver trainings in community settings where people feel comfortable‚Äîencampments, community centers, libraries, parks, and mobile outreach locations. We use plain language, hands-on practice, and real-world scenarios. Our trainers include people with lived experience who bring authentic understanding and credibility. When education is accessible, culturally informed, and peer-led, it transforms from abstract information into practical, life-saving skills that people actually use.

Education is empowerment. When a person learns to recognize the signs of overdose and administer naloxone, they become capable of saving a life. When someone understands how fentanyl affects the drug supply and how to test for it, they can make more informed choices about their own safety. When a family member learns about substance use disorder and harm reduction principles, they can support their loved one with compassion rather than judgment. This knowledge ripples through communities, creating networks of care and mutual support that make everyone safer.

Since launching our education programs, we have trained over 2,500 community members in overdose response and naloxone administration. We have delivered more than 150 harm reduction workshops reaching people who use drugs, family members, healthcare providers, law enforcement, and community organizations. We have certified over 500 individuals as naloxone administrators, creating a network of trained responders throughout King and Pierce Counties. Every trained individual represents exponential impact‚Äîeach person who learns these skills can teach others, respond to emergencies, and advocate for harm reduction in their own networks.

Empowerment Through Knowledge

Education is not just about transmitting information‚Äîit is about building confidence, reducing fear, and creating communities of mutual support and collective care. When people learn harm reduction skills, they gain more than technical knowledge; they gain agency, hope, and the power to protect themselves and others.

Many people who attend our naloxone trainings have never been in a position to save a life before. They leave our workshops carrying naloxone kits and the knowledge that they can respond effectively in an emergency. This transformation from bystander to potential lifesaver is profound. People report feeling more connected to their communities, more confident in their ability to help, and more hopeful about the possibility of reducing overdose deaths. We have heard countless stories of training participants who went on to reverse overdoses and save lives within weeks of completing training.

Our harm reduction workshops challenge stigma and misinformation about substance use. Many participants arrive with fear, judgment, or misunderstanding about people who use drugs. Through education grounded in science, compassion, and the voices of people with lived experience, participants leave with greater empathy, reduced stigma, and practical knowledge about how to support people in their communities. This shift in perspective is essential for creating communities where people feel safe seeking help, accessing services, and pursuing recovery on their own terms.

For people with lived experience, our peer educator development programs offer pathways to employment, leadership, and community impact. We train people who have personal experience with substance use, homelessness, or mental health challenges to become certified trainers and educators. These individuals bring authenticity and credibility that no textbook or degree can replicate. When a peer educator shares their story of recovery and teaches naloxone administration, participants listen differently. Peer-led education reduces stigma, increases engagement, and creates role models who demonstrate that change is possible.

Professional trainings transform how systems and institutions engage with people who use drugs. Healthcare providers who complete our harm reduction training report feeling more comfortable and effective working with patients who use substances. Social workers learn strategies for building trust and meeting people where they are. Law enforcement officers gain understanding of Good Samaritan laws and how to support rather than criminalize people experiencing overdose. When professionals across sectors understand harm reduction, we create more compassionate, effective, and coordinated systems of care.

Education creates ripple effects throughout communities. A person trained in naloxone administration might teach their friends and family. A healthcare provider who completes harm reduction training might change protocols at their clinic. A peer educator who shares their recovery story might inspire someone else to seek treatment. A community organization that hosts a workshop might integrate harm reduction principles into all their programming. This is how education creates systemic change‚Äîone person, one organization, one community at a time.

How to Access Education & Training Services

All our education and training programs are free and open to community members, professionals, and organizations throughout King and Pierce Counties. We offer trainings in multiple formats to ensure accessibility for everyone who wants to learn.

For Individuals: Join our regularly scheduled naloxone training sessions and harm reduction workshops. We offer trainings at our mobile outreach locations, community centers, and partner sites throughout the region. Check our outreach schedule or contact us to find the next available training. Walk-ins are welcome, and no prior experience or registration is required.

For Groups and Organizations: Request a customized training for your organization, workplace, school, faith community, or neighborhood group. We can deliver naloxone certification, harm reduction workshops, professional development training, and community education programs tailored to your group's needs and interests. Trainings can be scheduled at your location or ours. Contact us to discuss your needs and schedule a session.

For Aspiring Peer Educators: Apply for our peer educator development program. We provide comprehensive training, certification, mentorship, and employment opportunities for people with lived experience who want to become harm reduction trainers. Contact us to learn about upcoming cohorts and application requirements.

For Professionals: Enroll in our professional development trainings designed for healthcare providers, social workers, law enforcement, and community organizations. We offer continuing education credits and specialized curricula focused on harm reduction, trauma-informed care, and cultural competency. Contact us for upcoming professional training dates and registration information.

Virtual Options: Can't attend in person? We offer virtual naloxone training, harm reduction education webinars, and online peer support groups. Virtual training includes mailed naloxone kits so you can practice and receive supplies at home. Contact us to register for upcoming virtual sessions.

Training Impact & Outcomes

Our education programs create measurable impact in communities we serve. Training participants report increased confidence in responding to overdoses, greater knowledge of harm reduction practices, and reduced stigma toward people who use drugs. Follow-up surveys show that over 85% of naloxone training participants carry naloxone after training, and more than 40% have used it to reverse an overdose within one year of certification.

Healthcare and social service professionals who complete our trainings report significant changes in their practice. Providers describe feeling more comfortable discussing substance use with patients, more confident offering harm reduction resources, and more effective building trust with people who use drugs. Organizations that host our trainings often integrate harm reduction principles into their standard protocols and procedures, creating lasting systemic change.

Peer educators trained through our programs have gone on to deliver hundreds of community trainings, reaching thousands of additional people with harm reduction knowledge. Many have secured employment as harm reduction specialists, community health workers, and peer navigators. Their leadership strengthens the entire harm reduction movement and ensures that people with lived experience remain at the center of program design and service delivery.

Education and training are investments in community health and safety. Every person trained is a potential lifesaver, advocate, and agent of change. Together, we are building a movement of informed, empowered, compassionate community members who refuse to accept preventable overdose deaths and who fight for dignity, evidence-based care, and health equity for all people.
